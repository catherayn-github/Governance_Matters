Effect of Audit Quality on Investment Efficiency

info Importance

Understanding the impact of audit quality on investment efficiency in holding firms in the Philippines is essential for several reasons. As investors or decision-makers, we rely on accurate and trustworthy financial information. The quality of audits directly affects the reliability of financial statements, influencing our investment choices. High audit quality ensures that a firm’s financial position is accurately represented, leading to better investment decisions. Investment efficiency is crucial for maximizing returns, and poor financial reporting can lead to suboptimal investment choices, affecting returns.

By understanding how audit quality impacts these decisions, we can better assess risk and return potential. In the Philippines, where local regulations and economic factors play a role, strong audits can help mitigate these risks. Poor audits may result in inefficiencies, misallocation of resources, or even fraud, which can harm both firms and the market. By exploring this relationship, we can advocate for stronger audits, fostering a more transparent financial environment and enabling more effective investment decisions.

trending_up Measurement

Our study used simple linear regression to test the effect of audit quality on investment efficiency.

lightbulb_outline Findings of the Study

Our findings show that audit quality, measured by audit tenure, size, and fee, does not significantly affect investment efficiency, as all p-values exceed the 0.05 threshold. Mediation analysis also reveals no significant indirect effects.

Prior research, such as Siregar & Nuryanah (2019), supports this, showing a negative association between financial reporting quality and investment efficiency in underinvestment cases due to stakeholder skepticism. Further analysis reveals no significant results for overinvestment, while underinvestment shows a negative relationship with audit quality.

Studies like Buobaker et al. (2018) highlight that audit quality’s impact may depend on firm growth, benefiting high-growth firms but not necessarily improving investment efficiency for all.

Overall, our results suggest that audit quality is not a key determinant of investment efficiency, pointing to the need to explore other factors that drive investment performance.
