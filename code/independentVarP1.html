Corporate Governance

info Definition

Corporate governance is a framework of rules, practices, and procedures that steers a company’s direction and management, ensuring accountability to its stakeholders, including shareholders, employees, customers, and the community. It emphasizes oversight, risk management, and strategic decision-making, setting it apart from the daily operational tasks managed by executives. (IMD, 2024)

trending_up Measurement

We measure corporate governance using the following sub-variables:

  • Ownership Concentration: Shareholders with ≥25% ownership
  • Board Size: Total number of board of directors
  • Board Independence: Number of independent directors
  • Audit Committee: Number of members

Statement of the Problem
question_answer Statement of the Problem #2

What is the corporate governance of listed holding companies on the Philippine Stock Exchange for the years 2017-2023 in terms of:

  • Ownership concentration;
  • Board size;
  • Board independence; and
  • Number of audit committee members?

Findings of the Study

  • trending_up Ownership Concentration

    The graph shows key trends in ownership concentration: a peak in 2018, a dip in 2019, recovery in 2020, and steady growth through 2023, indicating overall stability despite fluctuations. We see these shifts influenced by factors like policy changes, market dynamics, and economic conditions, offering insights into corporate governance and market behavior.

    In 2017-2018, rising ownership concentration aligned with domestic consumption growth, supported by remittance inflows, favorable inflation, improved employment, and the TRAIN law. The 2019 decline reflected global risks impacting corporate ownership, while the 2020 dip mirrored COVID-19’s economic fallout.

    By 2021, ownership concentration increased, driven by strong performances from major corporations like San Miguel Corporation.

    From 2021 to 2023, we observed rising ownership concentration as holding firms capitalized on growth opportunities through market expansion, infrastructure investments, and favorable economic conditions.

  • group Board Size

    From the 133 disclosures we reviewed, 93 (69.9%) showed that companies typically have boards with 6 to 10 members, while 40 (30.1%) reported boards of 11 to 15 members.

    This supports the trend of most companies maintaining smaller boards, which aligns with the Code’s principles. Smaller, more manageable boards enable active participation, effective decision-making, and compliance with governance requirements, including independent directors and committee structures.

  • account_balance Board Independence

    We observed a steady increase in board independence among listed holding companies from 2017 to 2023, reflecting our efforts to align with PSE and SEC corporate governance standards.

    Board independence peaked in 2023 with a mean of 1.0546 (SD: 0.31059), while 2017 recorded the lowest mean at 0.9059 (SD: 0.36572). The seven-year average was 0.9837 (SD: 0.31091), highlighting our progress in strengthening governance practices.

    The rise in 2017-2018 aligns with Corporate Governance guidelines, which recommend at least three independent directors or 30% board representation.

    This trend was also supported by strong stock market performance in 2017, reflecting growing investor confidence and the demand for better governance.

    However, in 2022, we saw a decline in independent directors as companies focused on survival rather than governance reforms due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

  • assessment Audit Committee Members

    The graph shows a gradual increase in the average number of audit committee members from 2017 to 2023.

    From 2017 to 2019, we observed that the average remained stable at around 1.27 members, then rose slightly to 1.28 in 2020, reflecting our efforts to strengthen audit committees.

    In 2021, there was a temporary dip, likely due to challenges from the COVID-19 pandemic, followed by a sharp rise, peaking at 1.31 in 2023.

    We believe the decline in 2021 was due to pandemic-related pressures, such as increased workloads, resource constraints, and personal challenges faced by committee members.

    On the other hand, the overall upward trend aligns with regulatory standards like the Sarbanes-Oxley Act, highlighting the importance of independent and well-structured audit committees for improved financial transparency and governance.
